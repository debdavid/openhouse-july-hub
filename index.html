Four things that improve every agent interaction

Lead with the outcome

Not "Create a progress note." Instead: "Create an NDIS progress note for [participant] working on Goal 2. Observations: [notes]. Three paragraphs, plain English, no jargon."

Name the audience every time

Even with audience context in your system instruction, naming the specific audience sharpens the output. "Audience: family members — warm, plain English, no clinical terminology."

Give the most important thing first

"The key finding is a 22% increase in response time. Build the status update narrative around what this means for the project timeline."

Use the quality check as a prompt

"Review this output. Does it feel generic or specific to my context? Is there anything a professional in my field would find inaccurate? List what to fix, then rewrite."

Before using any output professionally — five questions

QuestionWhat to check
Does it serve the stated aim?If you removed a section, would the audience miss anything essential? If not, cut it.
Does the structure make sense?Does it build logically toward the conclusion? Would a sceptical reader find it convincing?
Is the language right for this audience?Read one section aloud as if you are the least familiar person in the room. Is it still clear?
Are there any risks?Unverified claims, legally sensitive content, anything that could mislead. The agent does not know your legal obligations — you do.
Does it meet your professional standard?Consistent format, appropriate length, correct terminology. Verify the standards you described were followed.

Healthcare & clinical services
Nothing Gets Missed at Handover
Clinical staff · Allied health · Nursing
Process
Review patient notes before shift end. Compile actions, medication changes, and alerts for incoming staff.
Pain
Quality varies with how rushed the shift has been. Critical information sometimes missed.
Sources
Shift observations, medication updates, flagged concerns, ward context
Produces
Clear handover summary flagging priority patients, pending actions, and clinical alerts
Human
Clinical judgment, escalation decisions, final accuracy check
