Part 1
Discover your opportunity.
Work through A–D to identify and confirm your task before moving to Part 2.
A.List 2–3 tasks you do most regularly that feel repetitive, time-consuming, or inconsistently done:
1. Processing my inbox every morning — reading emails and Teams messages, identifying what needs action, drafting responses. Takes 30–45 minutes and I often miss things when busy. 2. Writing client follow-up emails after meetings — always from scratch, quality varies depending on time. 3. Tracking open commitments and deadlines — I rely on memory and scattered notes, things fall through the gaps.
B.For the task you are considering, tick which apply:
C.How much time do you currently spend on this task?
30–45 minutes every working morning. Approximately 2.5–3.5 hours per week.
D.The task I am designing my digital colleague for:
My task
Daily email and Teams message review — commitment tracking, deadline identification, and action briefing
Part 2
Design your digital colleague.
Work through sections 1–7 in order. Be as specific as you can — vague inputs produce vague outputs.
1 · Agent name
Specific to your role and task
Never Miss a Deadline
Who uses it and how often?
Your role and frequency of use
Used by me — a self-employed business development professional. Every working morning, first thing.
2 · Current process and pain points
Walk through your steps from start to finish. Where is it slow, inconsistent, or frustrating? What happens when you are rushed?
Every morning I open Outlook and Microsoft Teams. I manually read through overnight and early messages — emails from clients, prospects, partners, and Teams direct messages and channel messages. I try to identify: what needs a response today, what can wait, what has a deadline attached. This decision-making is mentally draining before the real work starts. I have no reliable system for tracking what I have committed to. I rely on memory and unread email flags. Deadlines slip. Follow-ups get missed. Commitments I made get forgotten until someone chases me. Pain points: time-consuming manual sorting, no consistent method, missed commitments, reactive rather than proactive.
3 · Decision points
What decisions do you currently make manually that your digital colleague could help structure? List the judgments involved in this task.
- Which emails and messages require a response today vs this week vs never? - Which messages contain a commitment I have made (explicit or implied)? - Which items have a deadline attached — explicit or inferred? - Which items are blocked on someone else and need a follow-up? - Which items need to be escalated or flagged as high risk? - Which items are FYI only — no action required? - What is the single most important thing I need to action first?
4 · Knowledge sources
List every source the agent needs — Outlook, Teams, SharePoint, uploaded documents. If it is not listed here, the agent will not know to use it.
- Outlook inbox (emails from last 24 hours) - Microsoft Teams (direct messages and relevant channel messages) - Calendar (meeting context relevant to communications) - Key contacts document: VIP clients, hot prospects, important partners (uploaded) - Current deals and projects summary (uploaded) - My professional context: business development, building partnerships and converting prospects - My communication style: professional, direct, warm — not overly formal - My sign-off: Deb
5 · What the agent should produce
Describe the output format specifically — structure, length, sections, tone.
A structured daily action briefing with the following sections: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY Top 3 priorities, key risks, important deadlines, items that can wait. IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (P0/P1) Table: Priority · Action · Owner · Stakeholder · Deadline · Status · Evidence Source OPEN COMMITMENTS All confirmed and likely commitments I have made. Confirmed and likely clearly distinguished. RISKS AND DEPENDENCIES Delivery risks, conflicting commitments, missing approvals, blocked items. RECOMMENDED NEXT ACTIONS The three most important things to do first, in priority order. COVERAGE Reporting period and sources reviewed. Any access limitations noted.
6 · Non-negotiables — what the human still decides
What professional judgment must stay with you? What must this agent never do, regardless of what it finds?
I review and approve everything before any action is taken. The agent analyses and surfaces — it does not decide or act on my behalf. The agent must never: send messages, make commitments, take actions, fabricate deadlines, or present inferred information as confirmed fact. The agent must always: clearly distinguish between Confirmed (directly evidenced), Likely (strongly supported by context), and Possible (requires my verification). I decide: what actually gets done, what gets escalated, what gets ignored, and how to respond to everything surfaced.
7 · Success looks like...
How will you know this digital colleague is working? Be specific — time saved, quality improved, decisions improved.
My morning review takes under 10 minutes instead of 45. I have a clear, prioritised action list before I start any other work. I no longer miss commitments or deadlines because they were buried in email. I feel in control of my workload rather than reactive to it. The briefing is factual and evidence-based — not speculative.
